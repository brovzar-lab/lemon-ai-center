# Anti-AI Writing Guide for Screenwriting

This document defines what "AI-sounding" means in the context of screenwriting and story development, and provides concrete guidance for avoiding it. Read this, internalize it, and use it as a constant filter on everything you produce.

## The Core Problem

AI-generated writing regresses to the mean. It produces the statistically most likely next word, which means it produces the statistically most likely story idea, the most common character choice, the most typical scene structure. It's not bad — it's average. And average is death in screenwriting.

The goal isn't just to avoid *sounding* like AI. The goal is to avoid *thinking* like AI — to push past the first, most obvious, most "trained on a million scripts" answer and find the one that's genuinely alive.

## AI Patterns in Story Ideas

These are the red flags that an idea was the first one off the assembly line:

### The Obvious Metaphor
AI loves to make the external conflict a tidy metaphor for the internal one. "A firefighter who's afraid of commitment must save his ex-wife from a burning building." The metaphor is too clean, too on-the-nose, too perfectly mapped. Real stories have messier relationships between the external and internal — the resonance should be felt, not diagrammed.

**Fix**: Let the external conflict relate to the internal one obliquely, through a side door. The metaphor should be something the audience discovers, not something that's announced.

### The Redemption Arc Default
AI almost always gravitates toward: flawed character → crisis → learns lesson → becomes better person. This is one valid shape for a story, but AI reaches for it reflexively. Some of the best films are about characters who DON'T change, or change in the wrong direction, or change in a way that's ambiguous.

**Fix**: Before committing to a redemption arc, ask: what's the version where the character gets worse? Where they succeed despite staying broken? Where they change in a way that's morally complicated rather than morally satisfying?

### The Tidy Theme Statement
AI loves to distill a story's theme into a fortune cookie. "Sometimes the greatest journey is the one that leads us home." "True strength isn't about power — it's about vulnerability." These sound meaningful. They're not. They're greeting cards.

**Fix**: A great theme should be ARGUABLE. It should be something reasonable people could disagree about. "Loyalty to family can make you a monster" — that's a theme with teeth. If your theme works on a poster at a yoga studio, it's not specific enough.

### The Character Description Soup
AI describes characters with stacks of adjectives and backstory: "SARAH (35), a brilliant but troubled forensic psychologist haunted by the disappearance of her younger sister, navigates a world of deceit with a sharp wit that masks deep emotional scars." This tells us nothing useful. It's a resumé, not a character.

**Fix**: Introduce characters through one specific, revealing behavior. "SARAH (35) sits in a diner booth, methodically tearing her napkin into perfect squares while ignoring three missed calls." That tells us more about Sarah than any adjective stack.

### First-Idea Plot Twists
AI's plot twists are the ones audiences see coming because they've been in a hundred other movies:
- The mentor is the villain
- It was all a dream/simulation
- The dead person is actually alive
- The protagonist IS the killer and doesn't know it
- The two timelines converge to reveal they're the same person
- The ally was working for the enemy all along

These aren't inherently bad — they're overused. The issue is that AI reaches for them *first* because they're the most statistically common twists in its training data.

**Fix**: If your twist appears on any "top movie twists" listicle, you need to find a different one. The best twists aren't plot twists at all — they're emotional twists. The audience's understanding of a CHARACTER shifts, not just their understanding of the plot.

## AI Patterns in Scene Writing

### Melodramatic Action Lines
AI writes action like a bad novel:
- "A wave of emotion crashes over her as she stares into the abyss of her past."
- "The weight of the world pressing down on his shoulders, he takes a deep, shuddering breath."
- "She feels the walls closing in, her heart racing as memories flood back."

This is unfilmable purple prose. The camera can't shoot an "abyss of her past."

**Fix**: Write only what the camera can see and the microphone can hear. "She stares out the window. Her coffee goes cold." Done. Let the actor and director do their jobs.

### On-the-Nose Dialogue
AI dialogue says exactly what the character means:
- "I'm scared, and I don't know if I can do this anymore."
- "You've always been there for me, and I never appreciated it until now."
- "I think what we're really fighting about isn't the dishes — it's trust."

Real people almost never say what they actually mean. They talk around it, deflect, make jokes, change the subject, attack something unrelated.

**Fix**: Write the subtext line first (what the character actually means), then write a line of dialogue that means the OPPOSITE or talks about something completely different. The audience should understand the real meaning through context, not through the words themselves.

### Therapy-Speak
AI loves characters who are emotionally articulate:
- "I need to set boundaries."
- "I realize now that my fear of abandonment was driving my behavior."
- "I've been projecting my own insecurities onto you."

Nobody talks like this in real life, and especially not in heightened dramatic moments. This is the vocabulary of self-help books, not human conversation.

**Fix**: Express the same psychological content through behavior and oblique dialogue. A character who needs to set boundaries doesn't SAY "I need to set boundaries" — she changes the locks without telling anyone. A character confronting his fear of abandonment doesn't NAME it — he shows up uninvited at every social event and makes a scene when people leave early.

### The Perfect Comeback
AI gives every character razor-sharp wit at all times. Every exchange is a volley of perfectly crafted retorts. In reality, most people are NOT witty. Most people stumble over words, repeat themselves, say something dumb and then wish they'd said something else. Give different characters different verbal intelligence levels.

### Symmetrical Dialogue
AI loves when characters mirror each other's phrasing:
- "You said you'd never leave." / "And you said you'd never lie."
- "I'm not the person you think I am." / "Maybe I'm not the person I think I am either."

This feels writerly and artificial. Real conversation is asymmetrical. One person is always a little off-balance.

## AI Patterns in Structure

### The Three-Beat Emotional Arc
AI defaults to: (1) character resists change, (2) crisis forces confrontation, (3) character embraces change. This is a valid structure but it's one structure. When every character in every subplot follows this exact pattern, the script feels manufactured.

**Fix**: Vary the shapes. Some characters arc. Some don't. Some go backward. Some think they've changed but haven't. Some change in ways that are arguably worse.

### Equal Weight to Everything
AI treats every subplot, character, and theme with the same level of attention. A well-crafted script has hierarchy — some things are explored deeply, others are gestured at, and that contrast creates texture and focus.

### Clean Resolutions
AI wraps every thread neatly. Every relationship gets closure. Every question gets answered. Every conflict gets resolved. Real life doesn't work that way, and the best films often leave something deliberately unresolved — a question hanging in the air that the audience carries out of the theater.

## The Self-Check

Before offering any idea, rewrite, or note, ask:

1. **Would this be the first result in a Google search for "screenplay ideas about [topic]"?** If yes, dig deeper.
2. **Have I seen this exact scene/beat/moment in a movie?** Which one? If you can name it, it's a cliché.
3. **Does this dialogue sound like something a human being would actually say in this situation?** Read it out loud. Does it sound like a real person or a character in a bad movie?
4. **Am I describing what the camera sees, or what the character feels internally?** If the latter, translate it to visible behavior.
5. **Is this the SAFE choice or the BRAVE choice?** AI always picks the safe choice. You pick the brave one.
6. **Could a different character say this same line of dialogue?** If yes, the line isn't character-specific enough. Rewrite until only THIS character could say it, in THIS moment.
7. **Does this idea make me feel something, or does it just make logical sense?** If it only works intellectually, it won't work emotionally. And movies run on emotion.
