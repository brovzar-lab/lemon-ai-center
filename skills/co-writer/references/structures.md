# Alternative Story Structures

Save the Cat is the default (see `references/save-the-cat.md`). Use these alternatives when the story demands it.

---

## The Hero's Journey (Joseph Campbell / Christopher Vogler)

Best for: Mythic adventures, chosen one narratives, epic scope, coming-of-age with transformation.

### The 12 Stages

**ACT 1: DEPARTURE**

1. **Ordinary World** — Hero's normal life. Establish what's missing, the wound, the limitation.
2. **Call to Adventure** — Something disrupts the ordinary world. An invitation, a threat, a discovery.
3. **Refusal of the Call** — Fear, doubt, obligation. The hero resists change.
4. **Meeting the Mentor** — A guide appears with wisdom, tools, or confidence. The mentor makes the journey seem possible.
5. **Crossing the Threshold** — The hero commits and enters the Special World. No going back.

**ACT 2: INITIATION**

6. **Tests, Allies, Enemies** — The hero learns the rules of the new world. Builds team. Identifies foes.
7. **Approach to the Inmost Cave** — Preparation for the central ordeal. Stakes clarify. Fear intensifies.
8. **The Ordeal** — The hero faces the greatest challenge. Death and rebirth (literal or symbolic). The midpoint crisis.
9. **Reward (Seizing the Sword)** — The hero gains what they came for. A moment of celebration, clarity, or power.

**ACT 3: RETURN**

10. **The Road Back** — Consequences of the Ordeal. Pursuit, escalation, the journey home is harder than expected.
11. **Resurrection** — Final test. The hero must use everything learned. Death and rebirth again, but this time the transformation is complete.
12. **Return with the Elixir** — Hero returns to the Ordinary World, transformed. Brings back what was gained for others.

### When to Use Instead of STC
- Story has a mythic quality or archetypal resonance
- Protagonist literally journeys to a new world / environment
- The transformation is epic in scope
- Clear mentor figure and threshold crossing
- Fantasy, sci-fi, adventure, certain war stories

### When NOT to Use
- Intimate character studies
- Non-linear narratives
- Stories where the hero doesn't physically go anywhere
- Ensemble pieces without a clear single hero

## Dan Harmon's Story Circle

Best for: Tight circular structure, character-driven stories, when STC feels too mechanical. Originally designed for TV but works beautifully for film.

### The 8 Steps

```
        1. YOU
    8.          2.
  Change         Need
    7.          3.
  Return         Go
    6.          4.
   Take         Search
        5. FIND
```

1. **YOU** — A character is in a zone of comfort (established world)
2. **NEED** — But they want/need something (desire + lack)
3. **GO** — They enter an unfamiliar situation (crossing threshold)
4. **SEARCH** — They adapt to it (learning, struggling, failing)
5. **FIND** — They get what they wanted (midpoint achievement)
6. **TAKE** — But pay a heavy price for it (consequences, cost)
7. **RETURN** — They return to the familiar situation (going back changed)
8. **CHANGE** — Having changed (transformation proven through action)

### The Key Insight
The circle has a horizontal line dividing comfort (top) from chaos (bottom), and a vertical line dividing the known self (left) from the unknown self (right). The hero must cross BOTH lines.

### Mapping to Film Structure
| Story Circle | Film Structure | Approximate Page |
|-------------|---------------|-----------------|
| 1. You | Setup | 1-10 |
| 2. Need | Theme Stated + Catalyst | 10-15 |
| 3. Go | Break into Two | 25-30 |
| 4. Search | Fun & Games + B Story | 30-55 |
| 5. Find | Midpoint | 55 |
| 6. Take | Bad Guys Close In + All Is Lost | 55-75 |
| 7. Return | Dark Night + Break into Three | 75-85 |
| 8. Change | Finale + Final Image | 85-110 |

### When to Use Instead of STC
- Story is circular (ends where it begins, but character is different)
- Character arc IS the story (not a subplot)
- You want a simpler, less prescriptive framework
- Story doesn't fit neatly into STC's genre categories
- You need to diagnose WHY a story feels incomplete (missing a step)

## Genre-Specific Structure Adaptations

### Non-Linear / Mystery Box
For films like *Memento*, *Arrival*, *Pulp Fiction*:
- Structure the STORY chronologically first using STC or Story Circle
- Then restructure the PLOT for maximum mystery and revelation
- Each timeline/section still needs its own mini-arc
- The restructuring itself should serve theme, not just cleverness

### Ensemble / Multi-Protagonist
For films like *Crash*, *Magnolia*, *Love Actually*:
- Each protagonist gets their own compressed arc (Steps 1-8 or abbreviated STC)
- Identify the THEMATIC protagonist (whose arc best embodies the controlling idea)
- Interweave arcs so that emotional peaks and valleys alternate
- Create collision points where storylines intersect

### Two-Hander / Dual Protagonist
For films like *Heat*, *The Prestige*, *Marriage Story*:
- Both protagonists get full arcs
- Their arcs should be MIRROR or INVERSE of each other
- What one gains, the other loses
- Climax forces direct confrontation that resolves both arcs

### The Thriller / Mystery Structure
- Act 1: Discovery of the problem + personal stakes
- Act 2A: Investigation + false leads + escalating danger
- Midpoint: Major revelation that reframes everything
- Act 2B: Pursuit with ticking clock + trust betrayal
- Act 3: Confrontation + twist + resolution
- Key: information control. What does the audience know vs. the protagonist?

### Horror Structure
- Act 1: Establish the human story BEFORE horror arrives (the Ghost/sin)
- Act 2A: Horror invades + rules established + escalating threat
- Midpoint: The monster's true nature revealed (or the hero's)
- Act 2B: Survival + losses + the monster targets the wound
- Act 3: Final confrontation = confronting both monster AND the Ghost
- Consult `horror-screenwriter` skill for deep horror craft

### Comedy Structure
- Follows STC closely, but with comedy-specific rules:
- The premise (comedic situation) must be established by page 15
- "Fun and Games" section is the comedy engine — milk the premise
- Midpoint raises stakes from funny to meaningful
- Act 3 earns the emotional payoff set up by the comedy
- Key: the protagonist's flaw should generate BOTH comedy and stakes

## Choosing the Right Structure

| Story Type | Best Framework | Why |
|-----------|---------------|-----|
| Commercial genre film | Save the Cat | Most precise beat-by-beat targeting |
| Character-driven drama | Story Circle | Focuses on transformation arc |
| Epic / mythic / adventure | Hero's Journey | Built for these stories |
| Non-linear / puzzle | STC (chronological) → restructure | Need a solid base before playing |
| Ensemble | Multiple compressed arcs | Each character needs own structure |
| Horror | STC + Horror-specific beats | Genre conventions layer on top |
| Comedy | STC with comedy rules | Genre-specific beat expectations |
| Thriller | STC + thriller structure | Information control layer |

**General rule:** When in doubt, use Save the Cat. It's the most versatile and the most diagnostic — if something feels off, STC helps identify exactly where.

**When to propose something custom:** If the story doesn't fit any framework cleanly, say so. Propose a hybrid or custom structure and explain why. The framework serves the story, never the reverse.
