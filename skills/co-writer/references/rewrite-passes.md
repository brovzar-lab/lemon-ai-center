# Rewrite Passes & Scene Surgery

## The 7 Rewrite Passes

Sequential passes — each addresses one dimension. Don't try to fix everything at once.

---

### Pass 1: Structure

**Goal:** Every scene earns its place. Architecture is sound.

**Process:**
1. One-line summary for every scene
2. For each: "If I cut this, does the story still work?" If yes → cut or merge
3. Check act breaks — are they in the right places?
4. Verify escalation — does each sequence raise stakes?

**Red Flags:**
- Scene exists only for exposition (merge into action scene)
- Two scenes achieve same story purpose (pick the stronger one)
- No scene turns (nothing changes from start to end)
- Protagonist absent for 10+ pages

---

### Pass 2: Character

**Goal:** Arcs earned, consistent, emotionally resonant.

**Process:**
1. Track protagonist's WANT and NEED across every act
2. Verify transformation milestones (when does change start?)
3. Check: Does protagonist make the story happen, or does it happen to them?
4. Ensure supporting characters have their own agendas (not just serving plot)
5. Verify antagonist has legitimate POV

**Key Questions:**
- Is the character active (making choices) or passive?
- At what point does transformation begin?
- Is change earned or sudden?
- What scene is missing to make the arc feel complete?
- At least one moment of surprising vulnerability AND one of surprising strength?

---

### Pass 3: Dialogue

**Goal:** Every line sounds like the character speaking, contains subtext, serves the scene.

**The Dialogue Audit (per scene):**
1. Cover character names — can you tell who's speaking? NO → rewrite for distinct voices
2. Any line pure exposition? YES → dramatize the information
3. Any character says exactly what they feel? YES → add subtext, talk AROUND it
4. Any speech longer than 4 lines? YES → break up, add interruptions, or cut
5. Would removing this exchange lose anything? NO → cut it

---

### Pass 4: Tension

**Goal:** Screenplay never lets audience relax in the wrong places.

**Tension Mapping:**
Rate each scene 1-10. Rules:
- Never below 4 for more than 2 consecutive scenes
- Overall trajectory escalates (Act 3 > Act 1)
- After a 9-10 moment, one scene of 5-6 gives audience air
- Two consecutive low-tension scenes = pacing problem

**Tension Tools:**
- Dramatic irony (audience knows something character doesn't)
- Ticking clocks (time pressure)
- Competing agendas (multiple characters want different things)
- Unspoken threats (what MIGHT happen > what does)
- Planted questions (audience needs answers)

---

### Pass 5: Theme

**Goal:** Screenplay says something through story, not speeches.

**Theme Check:**
1. State controlling idea in one sentence
2. Does climax prove or disprove this idea?
3. Does EVERY major subplot echo theme from a different angle?
4. Is theme expressed through CHARACTER CHOICES, not dialogue?
5. Would removing any thematic reference lose meaning?

---

### Pass 6: Visual

**Goal:** This is a MOVIE, not a filmed play.

**Visual Audit:**
- 5 images from this screenplay that haunt the viewer?
- Any important scene just two people talking in a room? Add physical action.
- Environments used to externalize emotion?
- Visual motifs that recur and evolve?
- Would the story work as radio play? (If yes, not visual enough)

**Fix:** Cut dialogue by 40%, replace with visual beats. Characters DOING something while talking. Location contributing to mood.

---

### Pass 7: Polish

**Goal:** Every word earns its place. Professional presentation.

**Checklist:**
- Action lines: active voice, present tense
- No paragraph longer than 3 lines
- First mention of characters = CAPS
- Consistent character names
- No typos, grammar, formatting issues
- Page count: 90-120 for features
- Sound design in CAPS ("A GUNSHOT echoes")
- No redundant parentheticals
- Scene headers consistent (INT./EXT. LOCATION - TIME)

## Scene Surgery Protocol

When a scene isn't working and you're not sure why.

### 5-Question Diagnostic

1. **What's the scene ABOUT?** (Not plot — emotion)
2. **What CHANGES?** (Value shift from start to end)
3. **Where's the CONFLICT?** (Who wants what from whom?)
4. **Where's the TURN?** (The moment it shifts)
5. **What does the audience KNOW?** (Information advantage/disadvantage)

**If you can't answer these → the scene needs rewriting from concept, not polishing.**

### BMOC Diagnostic (Layer on top)
After Scene Surgery, apply BMOC analysis:
- What's the Beat Question?
- Where are the B/M/O/C crescendos?
- Are they choices or information?
- Is antagonist power comparable to protagonist?

## The "Kill Your Darlings" Method

### Decision Framework
```
Does this moment serve the STORY or the WRITER?
├── STORY: Advances plot, develops character, deepens theme
│   └── KEEP IT
└── WRITER: It's clever, well-written, personally meaningful
    ├── Does removing it hurt the story?
    │   ├── YES → KEEP (serves both)
    │   └── NO → CUT (save in "darlings" file)
    └── Can you use the best part elsewhere?
        ├── YES → TRANSPLANT
        └── NO → CUT
```

### Cutting Priorities (when reducing page count)
1. Scenes duplicating information given elsewhere
2. Subplots not connected to main theme
3. Dialogue exchanges running longer than necessary
4. Action sequences that don't escalate stakes
5. Transitions that can be compressed

## Tone Consistency Audit

### Common Problems
- Comedy scene in thriller breaks tension accidentally
- Tonal whiplash between scenes
- Inconsistent violence level (stylized then suddenly realistic)
- Character humor level changing without reason

Flag any scene that breaks the tonal contract. Suggest adjustment that preserves story function.

---

## Subtext Elevation Techniques

1. **Object Language** — Characters communicate through objects: giving, withholding, breaking, protecting things
2. **Physical Space** — Proximity = emotional closeness. Moving closer/farther reveals relationship shifts
3. **The Unfinished Sentence** — What characters DON'T finish reveals more than what they do
4. **Deflection Dialogue** — Characters answer different questions than asked
5. **The Loaded Ordinary** — Ordinary activities (cooking, driving) become charged with unspoken meaning

Apply at least 2 techniques to any scene with subtext problems. Surface conversation should seem normal; emotional undercurrent clearly felt but never explicitly stated.

## Pacing Diagnostics

### Scene Length Balance
- Under 1 page: Good for transitions, multiple in a row = choppy
- 1-3 pages: Ideal for most scenes
- 3-5 pages: Major scenes only (confrontations, set pieces)
- Over 5 pages: Almost certainly needs cutting

### Sequence Pacing
Group scenes into sequences (5-8 scenes each). Each sequence should:
1. Open with a mini-hook
2. Build through complications
3. End with a mini-climax launching the next sequence

### The Page-Turn Test
Read and note every point where you'd stop reading. Each = pacing problem needing a hook, twist, or question planted before it.
