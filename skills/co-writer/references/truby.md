# John Truby's Anatomy of Story

For deep Truby analysis including genre-specific beats from "Anatomy of Genres," consult the `truby-expert` skill. This reference covers the core methodology for quick access.

## Core Philosophy

Story is organic, not mechanical. Every element connects to every other. The story grows from a single seed: the **moral argument** — what the story is really about beneath the plot.

"Don't tell the audience a story. Give them an argument about how to live."

---

## The 7 Key Structure Steps (Foundation)

These are the load-bearing walls. If these are weak, nothing else matters.

### 1. Weakness/Need
The hero has a moral AND psychological weakness hurting them and others. They don't know it yet.

- **Psychological need:** What the hero must learn to have a fulfilling life
- **Moral need:** What the hero must learn to treat others properly
- Both must be present. Psychological alone = therapy. Moral alone = sermon.

### 2. Desire (The Want)
A specific, concrete goal. The story's spine — everything hangs on it.

- Must be specific enough to track ("win the case," not "find happiness")
- Creates the throughline: will they get it or not?

### 3. Opponent (The Necessary Antagonist)
Someone who wants the SAME goal as the hero — or whose goal directly conflicts.

- Best opponents compete for the same thing (same desire, different approach)
- Should target the hero's specific weakness
- Must have a legitimate point of view

### 4. Plan
The hero's strategy for achieving the desire. Drives Act 2.

- Specific enough for the audience to track progress
- Has steps that can be complicated or blocked

### 5. Battle
Final conflict between hero and main opponent. The climax.

- Strongest confrontation in the story
- Both sides at full power
- The moral argument is decided here

### 6. Self-Revelation
The hero sees their weakness/need for the first time.

- **Psychological:** "I see how I've been living wrong"
- **Moral:** "I see how I've been hurting others"
- Must be EARNED, not given by a mentor or speech

### 7. New Equilibrium
The hero's world after battle and self-revelation. Changed irreversibly.

- The hero sees reality differently
- The moral argument has been proven or disproven

## The Moral Argument

Truby's most distinctive contribution: every story makes an argument about how to live.

### Building the Moral Argument

**Step 1: Define the Moral Problem**
What is the hero doing wrong to others at the start? This is NOT a flaw for the audience to sympathize with — it's actual harm the hero causes.

**Step 2: Define the Moral Opponent**
The opponent represents a different (often worse) approach to the same moral problem. They force the hero to see the consequences of their path.

**Step 3: Build the Moral Web**
Every major character embodies a different approach to the central moral problem. The story becomes a debate played out through action and consequence.

**Step 4: The Moral Self-Revelation**
The hero doesn't just learn a lesson — they see specifically how they've been hurting others. This must be dramatized through action, not stated.

### Moral Argument Template
```
MORAL PROBLEM: [What ethical question does the story explore?]
HERO'S APPROACH: [How does the hero handle this problem — wrongly?]
OPPONENT'S APPROACH: [How does the opponent handle it — differently wrong?]
OTHER CHARACTERS: [Each embodies a variation on the moral question]
MORAL REVELATION: [What does the hero finally see about how to treat others?]
CONTROLLING IDEA: [The story proves: "When you [action], you get [consequence]"]
```

## The Character Web

Truby's key insight: characters are not created in isolation — they are a SYSTEM of relationships where each character defines the others through comparison and contrast.

### The Four-Corner Opposition
Create a web of at least four characters, each representing a different approach to the central moral problem:

```
        HERO ←————→ MAIN OPPONENT
         ↕                ↕
    ALLY/FRIEND ←——→ FAKE-ALLY OPPONENT
```

- **Hero:** Wrong approach, learns through the story
- **Main Opponent:** Different wrong approach, may or may not learn
- **Ally:** Embodies what the hero could be (hope)
- **Fake-Ally Opponent:** Appears to share hero's values, actually undermines them

### Building the Web
1. Start with the moral problem
2. Give each character a DIFFERENT approach to that problem
3. Make them compete — forcing the audience to weigh the approaches
4. The story resolves by showing which approach is right (or least wrong)

### Character Web Template
```
MORAL PROBLEM: [Central ethical question]

HERO:
  Approach: [How they handle the moral problem]
  Weakness: [What they can't see about their approach]
  What they learn: [Self-revelation]

MAIN OPPONENT:
  Approach: [Their competing answer to the moral problem]
  Strength: [What's legitimate about their view]
  Mirror to hero: [How they reflect the hero's worst self]

ALLY:
  Approach: [Different angle on the moral problem]
  Function: [How they challenge/support the hero]
  
FAKE-ALLY OPPONENT:
  Approach: [Appears aligned with hero, actually different]
  Function: [How they tempt or mislead the hero]
```

## Truby's Scene Construction

### The Scene Sequence
Truby views scenes as part of a larger "desire line" — each scene is a step in the hero's pursuit of their desire:

1. **Desire:** What does the hero want in this scene?
2. **Endpoint:** Where does the scene end up?
3. **Opponent:** Who blocks the hero?
4. **Plan:** What strategy does the hero use?
5. **Conflict:** The clash of strategies
6. **Twist/Revelation:** What new information changes things?
7. **New plan or adjusted desire:** How the scene launches the next one

### Revelation Sequences
Truby's specialty — stories built on cascading revelations:
- Each revelation reframes what came before
- The audience's understanding deepens with each reveal
- Best revelations change the MORAL meaning of events, not just plot facts

## Truby vs. Other Methodologies

### When to Use Truby Over Save the Cat
- Story needs a stronger thematic/moral spine
- Multiple characters need interlocking moral journeys
- Story is built on revelations and shifting understanding
- You want deeper exploration of WHY characters do what they do
- Genre: Mystery, thriller, drama with moral complexity

### When to Use Truby Over Hero's Journey
- Story is not mythic/adventure
- Hero's journey is too linear for the story's needs
- Story is about moral growth, not heroic triumph
- Urban, contemporary, or relationship-driven stories

### Truby + Enneagram Integration
Truby's weakness/need maps directly to Enneagram:
- **Weakness** = Core Fear + Survival Strategy (the Lie in action)
- **Psychological need** = Growth direction (what they must embrace)
- **Moral need** = How the core fear causes harm to others
- **Self-revelation** = Seeing the fear for what it is

### Truby + Story Grid Integration
Truby's moral argument maps to Story Grid's controlling idea:
- Both define what the story PROVES through its climax
- Story Grid's obligatory scenes often align with Truby's 22 steps
- Use Story Grid for genre contract validation, Truby for moral depth
