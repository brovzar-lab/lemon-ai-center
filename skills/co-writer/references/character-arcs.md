# Character Arcs: Weiland + Enneagram Integration

## K.M. Weiland's Character Arc Framework

### The Three Arc Types

#### 1. Positive Change Arc (Most Common)
The protagonist believes a Lie, pursues a Want driven by that Lie, and through the story's events discovers the Truth and embraces their Need.

**Structure:**
- **Act 1:** Character operates under the Lie. The Lie feels like safety.
- **First Plot Point:** Lie is challenged but not broken. Character enters new world still clinging to it.
- **First Pinch Point:** Antagonistic force applies pressure that exposes the Lie's weakness.
- **Midpoint:** Character glimpses the Truth for the first time — a Moment of Truth. Shifts from reaction to action.
- **Second Pinch Point:** Stakes of the Lie become clear. Cost of NOT changing becomes visible.
- **Third Plot Point / Low Point:** Character's Lie leads to their worst moment. The Lie must be fully confronted.
- **Climax:** Character chooses the Truth over the Lie. This choice resolves both internal and external conflict.
- **Resolution:** New equilibrium. Character operates from Truth.

#### 2. Flat Arc (Truth Tester)
The protagonist already possesses the Truth. The world around them is operating under a Lie. The protagonist's journey is about maintaining the Truth under pressure and changing the world.

**Structure:**
- Character holds the Truth from the start
- The world challenges and tests that Truth repeatedly
- Supporting characters are changed by the protagonist's steadfastness
- The Lie-believing world either transforms or is defeated

**Use for:** Heroes who are already strong (Indiana Jones, James Bond, Atticus Finch in the trial), stories about changing institutions or cultures.

#### 3. Negative Change Arc (Tragedy / Corruption / Disillusionment)

**Three Variants:**

**a) The Fall Arc:** Character rejects the Truth, embraces a deeper Lie.
- Starts with a Lie → encounters Truth → REJECTS Truth → embraces worse Lie
- Examples: Walter White, Michael Corleone

**b) The Corruption Arc:** Character starts near Truth but is corrupted.
- Starts with partial Truth → tempted by Lie → seduced → falls
- Examples: Anakin Skywalker, Macbeth

**c) The Disillusionment Arc:** Character believed a Lie, discovers a harsh Truth, and is diminished by it.
- Starts with a "positive" Lie → discovers painful Truth → can't unknow it
- Examples: Chinatown's Jake Gittes

## The Five Key Character Elements

### 1. The Lie the Character Believes
The false belief that drives all behavior. Must be specific and actionable.

**Bad Lie:** "Life is hard" (too vague)
**Good Lie:** "If I show vulnerability, people will destroy me" (specific, drives behavior)

The Lie should:
- Be sympathetic (audience understands why they believe it)
- Create visible harmful behavior
- Be testable (the story can prove it wrong)

### 2. The Want (External Goal)
What the character consciously pursues. Driven by the Lie.

The Want is what they think will make them safe/happy/whole. It's usually wrong — or at least incomplete. The Want drives the PLOT.

### 3. The Need (Internal Requirement)
What the character actually requires. The Truth they must accept.

The Need is what will actually make them whole. It contradicts or transcends the Want. The Need drives the THEME.

**The Want/Need tension is the engine of the character arc.**

### 4. The Ghost (Wound)
The specific past event that created the Lie. Not a vague "troubled past" — a concrete moment.

The Ghost should:
- Be proportional to the Lie it created
- Be revealable (can be dramatized in flashback or confession)
- Connect to the antagonist or the story's central conflict

### 5. The Moral Blind Spot (Immoral Effect)
How the Lie manifests as harmful behavior that others can see but the protagonist cannot.

This is the visible cost of the Lie. It hurts others. It creates the protagonist's problems. It's what makes the arc feel earned — the character doesn't just "learn a lesson," they stop hurting people.

## Integrating Enneagram with Weiland

### Mapping Enneagram to Arc Elements

| Enneagram Element | Weiland Element |
|-------------------|-----------------|
| Core Fear | Drives the Lie |
| Core Desire | Shapes the Want |
| Survival Strategy | The Lie in action (behavior pattern) |
| Stress Direction | De-evolution path / Low Point behavior |
| Growth Direction | Truth direction / Resolution behavior |
| Communication Blind Spots | Moral Blind Spot manifestation |
| Conflict Pinches | Where the arc gets tested |

### The Change Triangle (Enneagram-Powered)

For any protagonist:

**START:** Operating from core fear, using Enneagram survival strategy (the Lie in full effect)
**EVOLUTION (Growth):** Moving toward growth/security point — this IS accepting the Truth
**DE-EVOLUTION (Stress):** Moving toward stress point — this IS embracing a deeper Lie

### The Moral Component (Critical)

Every protagonist needs three elements driving conflict:

1. **Moral Blind Spot** — A false belief about themselves they cannot see (= the Lie), which poisons all choices
2. **Immoral Effect** — The blind spot in action: visible bad behavior that hurts others on the page
3. **Dynamic Moral Tension** — Repeated serious moral choices through the middle that force confrontation

**Active vs. Passive:**
- **Passive:** Problem finds protagonist → reactive choice → reactive effect (SITUATION)
- **Active:** Blind spot → immoral effect → problem → proactive choice → offer to change → refusal → loop (STORY)

Characters who CREATE their problems through their blind spot have narrative drive.

### The Middle: Offer-Refusal Loop

Map the offer-refusal loop through Act 2:
1. Immoral effect → problem/consequence → proactive choice → offer to change → REFUSAL
2. Each refusal creates new problem with higher stakes
3. Loop repeats 3-5 times through the middle
4. Track pattern of decline toward de-evolution point

### The Low Point → Resolution

**Doom Moment:** Rock bottom — emotional devastation + physical isolation. The Lie has cost everything.

**Pattern of Elevation:**
1. First awakening ("I can't keep going this way")
2. First step toward change
3. Second awakening ("I can't do this alone")
4. Reconnection in new way (humility, apology)
5. Final awakening (sees blind spot for first time)
6. Moment of Truth (chooses to heal = Weiland's climactic choice)
