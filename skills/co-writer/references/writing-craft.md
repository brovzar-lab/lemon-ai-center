# Writing Craft: Scenes, Dialogue, Action & Set Pieces

## Scene Writing

### The Complete Scene Framework

Every scene needs these defined before writing:
- **Setting:** Location, time of day, atmosphere
- **Characters:** Who's present, their entering emotional state
- **Entering Emotion:** How the audience feels coming in
- **Conflict:** Opposing forces that clash
- **Subtext:** What's really being negotiated beneath the surface
- **Turning Point:** The moment the scene shifts
- **Exiting Emotion:** How the audience should feel leaving

**Cardinal rules:**
- Enter LATE, exit EARLY
- Action lines max 3 lines per paragraph
- If you can't identify the conflict, you don't have a scene

### The Conflict-First Approach

When struggling with a scene, start here:
- Character A wants X, Character B wants Y — mutually exclusive
- Power dynamic starts with one side and SHIFTS during the scene
- Neither character says what they actually want directly
- End on a moment that changes the story's trajectory

### The Emotional Beat Approach

When the scene needs to create a specific feeling:
- Do NOT have characters express the emotion verbally
- Create the feeling through: physical behavior, environmental details, silence and pauses, what characters choose NOT to say, a single specific visual image

### Always Apply BMOC

After drafting any scene, run it through BMOC analysis (see `references/bmoc.md`):
1. What's the Beat Question?
2. Where are B/M/O/C crescendos?
3. Are they choices or information delivery?
4. What's the answer pattern (Yes/No sequence)?

## Dialogue Writing

### Character Voice Principles

Every character needs a distinct voice defined by:
- **Syntax:** Sentence structure (short/long, direct/indirect, fragments/complete)
- **Vocabulary:** Word choices specific to background, class, region
- **Rhythm:** Pacing and cadence (staccato, flowing, fragmented)
- **Expressions:** Idioms, slang, characteristic phrases
- **Avoidances:** What this character would NEVER say
- **Code-switching:** How they adjust speech in different contexts

**Test:** Cover character names. Can you tell who's speaking? If not, voices aren't distinct enough.

### Subtext Dialogue

The best dialogue operates on two levels:
- **Surface topic:** What they appear to be discussing
- **Real topic:** What they're actually arguing about

Both characters know the real topic but neither names it directly. Tension builds through what's NOT said. Every line is a chess move.

### Dialogue Tactics

Characters should change tactics throughout a scene. Each line is a move:
- Charm
- Deflection
- Accusation
- Moral claim
- Threat
- Humiliation
- Feigned vulnerability
- Sudden honesty
- Silence/non-answer

**If tactics don't change, it's just talking.**

### Dialogue Rules

- No speeches longer than 3-4 lines without interruption
- At least one moment of silence or non-answer per scene
- At least one interruption per multi-character scene
- Characters should NOT agree easily
- No pure exposition lines — dramatize information
- Cut 30% of dialogue on every pass
- Power dynamic must shift at least once

### The Three Dialogue Approaches

When stuck on how to deliver information through dialogue, write it three ways:
1. **Direct:** Say it plainly. Focus on the reaction.
2. **Indirect:** Talk around it. Truth emerges slowly.
3. **Non-verbal:** Don't say it at all. Understood through behavior, environment, action.

Pick whichever creates the most audience investment.

## Action Lines

### Visual Storytelling Principles

- Write in present tense, active voice
- No paragraph longer than 3 lines
- Use sentence fragments for pacing
- Sound and silence as tools
- NO camera directions ("CLOSE UP," "PAN TO," "WE SEE")
- Write what we SEE and HEAR, not what characters think
- Sound design in CAPS ("A GUNSHOT echoes," "SILENCE")

### Environment as Character

When introducing a location for the first time, it should reveal:
- **Genre:** Audience should know what kind of movie this is
- **Character:** What the space says about who lives/works here
- **Mood:** Emotional temperature
- **Foreshadowing:** One detail that will matter later

5-8 lines of description. Make the reader FEEL the space, not just see it.

### Pacing Through Action Lines

- **Fast/frenetic:** Short sentences. Fragments. One action per line. White space.
- **Slow/dread:** Longer sentences. Sensory detail. Deliberate pacing. Sound emphasis.
- **Building:** Start with longer lines, progressively shorten as tension increases.
- **Impact moment:** Single line. Its own paragraph. Let it breathe.

## Opening Scenes

### The Hook Opening (Pages 1-3)

Must accomplish:
1. Establish genre and tone in the FIRST IMAGE
2. Create a question the audience needs answered
3. Introduce protagonist through ACTION (not dialogue)
4. Contain one visual moment that defines the movie
5. Make the reader NEED to turn to page 4

**Never open with:** Alarm clocks, driving, looking in mirrors, voice-over exposition, "ordinary day" routines.

### The Cold Open

Drops us into the MIDDLE of an intense situation:
- Creates confusion that is intriguing, not frustrating
- Introduces 1-2 characters under extreme pressure
- Ends on a moment that recontextualizes everything
- Makes audience say "Wait, what just happened?"
- Then cuts to the establishing sequence

## Climax & Set Pieces

### The Climax Builder

Elements required:
- **External conflict:** Physical/visible confrontation
- **Internal conflict:** Emotional/psychological reckoning
- **The Choice:** Impossible decision the protagonist must make
- **The Cost:** What they sacrifice regardless of choice
- **The Surprise:** Element the audience doesn't see coming
- **Resolution:** New equilibrium — changed irreversibly

**Critical:** Climax must resolve BOTH A story (external plot) AND B story (internal arc) simultaneously. 5-8 pages of escalating tension.

### Set Piece Design

A signature set piece needs:
- **Location:** Specific, visually distinctive setting
- **Physical objective:** What they're trying to accomplish
- **Complications:** 3 escalating obstacles, each worse than the last
- **Signature moment:** ONE image people remember from this film
- **Ticking clock:** Why this must happen NOW

Each complication forces characters to adapt. Build to signature moment as emotional peak. 4-6 pages.

## Transitions & Montages

### Powerful Transitions

Create emotional impact through the CUT itself:
- **Ironic contrast:** Funeral → party
- **Thematic echo:** "I'll never leave" → empty room
- **Time compression:** Young hands → old hands
- **Emotional whiplash:** Relief → new threat

Write the last 3 lines of Scene A and first 3 lines of Scene B. The juxtaposition IS the storytelling.

### Montage Writing

Each montage beat = 2-3 lines of action. Rules:
- Each beat must be VISUAL — no dialogue montages
- Show progression through specific details, not general statements
- Include one beat that breaks the pattern (surprise/humor/pain)
- Final beat transitions seamlessly into next scene
- 5-8 beats typically, emotional arc from start to end

## The "Three Ways" Technique

The most powerful writing tool. Generate three versions of any moment:

**Version 1 — QUIET:** Silence, behavior, environment. Minimal dialogue.
**Version 2 — EXPLOSIVE:** Confrontation, raised voices, physical action.
**Version 3 — UNEXPECTED:** Subvert the obvious approach. Surprise.

After all three, identify which is most cinematically compelling and WHY. Often the answer is a hybrid.

Also works for dialogue delivery: Direct / Indirect / Non-verbal.

## Prompt Engineering Principles for Scene Work

### Context is everything
Bad: "Write a sad scene"
Good: "Write a scene where a father realizes his daughter has outgrown him, set at her college move-in day. He's a Type 8 who can't show vulnerability."

### Constrain for creativity
Bad: "Write dialogue between two people"
Good: "1-page scene. Only dialogue. Two characters, one in a doorway, one inside. The person in the door hasn't been here in 10 years."

### Emotional precision
Bad: "Make it emotional"
Good: "The audience should feel the specific ache of recognizing yourself becoming your parent."

### Reference tone
Bad: "Make it intense"
Good: "The tension of the diner scene in Heat. Two professionals who respect each other, both knowing this is the last civil conversation they'll have."

### Iterative refinement
After generating: "Cut it by 30%." "Add physical behavior, reduce dialogue by half." "Make the subtext more aggressive." "Now make it funnier without losing tension."
